Mathematical Platonism in the Age of Computation

Do mathematical objects exist independently of minds? The computer, finding proofs no human has seen, presses the ancient question with new force.

In 1976, Kenneth Appel and Wolfgang Haken announced that every planar map can be coloured with four colours so that no two adjacent regions share a hue. The proof was not, in the ordinary sense, a proof a person could read. It reduced the problem to some two thousand configurations and then set a computer to grind through them, case by mechanical case, for over a thousand hours. Mathematicians who had waited a century for the result found themselves holding a certificate they could not verify by inspection. The theorem was true; the reasons were stored on a tape. Something in the discipline shifted that year, and it has not shifted back.

The unease was philosophical before it was practical. For if the warrant for a theorem can live inside a machine, opaque to the very minds the proof was meant to convince, then we must ask what a proof was ever for, and what, exactly, it touches.

The realm and its trespassers

Platonism in mathematics is the conviction that its objects—the integers, the perfect circle, the uncountable cardinals—exist independently of us, neither invented nor located in space and time, and that to do mathematics is to discover what is already so. Kurt Gödel held some such view, and it lent his work a strange confidence: the continuum hypothesis had an answer, he believed, whether or not our axioms could reach it. Against him stands the long line of constructivists and formalists for whom a mathematical object is nothing until it is built, and a proof nothing but a finite arrangement of symbols obeying agreed rules.

The computer arrives as an awkward witness in this dispute, because it seems to take sides with both. When a machine establishes a result by exhausting cases no human could survey, it behaves as though it were surveying a terrain—reporting back from a country it did not make. Yet everything it does is finite manipulation, symbol pushed against symbol, the formalist's dream rendered in silicon. The same act looks like exploration to the realist and like bookkeeping to his opponent. What has changed is that the bookkeeping has outrun us.

What the machine cannot mean

Consider the asymmetry. A human proof, when we understand it, carries a sense of necessity: we see not merely that the thing is so but why it could not be otherwise. Appel and Haken's machine offered the first without the second. It delivered truth stripped of insight, a fact severed from its explanation. And this severance is precisely what discomfits the Platonist, who had always supposed that the abstract realm, if it existed, would be intelligible—that its objects were the kind of thing a mind could, in principle, behold.

A truth no one understands is still a truth, but it is a postcard from a place we are no longer sure anyone has been.

More recent systems sharpen the point. Interactive proof assistants now verify arguments line by formal line, and machine-search programs have settled questions, such as the Boolean Pythagorean triples problem, whose proofs run to hundreds of terabytes—objects no person will ever read in full. Here the formalist seems vindicated: correctness has become a property checkable without comprehension, a matter of mechanical assent. And yet the questions these machines answer were not invented by them. The triples were waiting. The colouring of maps was settled before the first computer hummed; the machine only found which settlement was the case.

The independence we wanted

There is an irony worth dwelling on. The Platonist's deepest wish was that mathematical reality be independent of any particular mind—that the truths hold whether or not we, with our specific and perishable cognition, ever grasp them. The computer grants this wish in a form almost too literal to bear. It shows us truths secured by no mind at all, intelligible to none, and yet, apparently, true. Independence from the human mind turns out to mean independence from understanding itself.

This is not a proof of Platonism; nothing here decides the old question, and perhaps nothing can. But it changes the texture of the asking. The realist once pointed to the felt inevitability of mathematical insight as evidence that we were in contact with something real. Now the contact and the insight have come apart. We can have the result without the seeing, the destination reported without the journey, the theorem true and the why withheld. If the abstract realm exists, the machine has begun mapping its far provinces and sending back only coordinates.

What we discover, when a machine discovers for us, may be exactly what the Platonist always claimed was there—a country that does not need us. We had only failed to notice how much we wanted it to.
