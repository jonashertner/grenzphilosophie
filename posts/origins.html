On Origins: Cosmological and Conceptual

Every account of a beginning presupposes what it sets out to explain. On the strange recursion of asking where anything, including the question, comes from.

Cosmology has a peculiar grammar. Its great achievement, the reconstruction of fourteen billion years of history from the faint static of microwave radiation and the recession of distant light, is also the site of its deepest embarrassment. Run the expansion backward, compress the galaxies into a denser and hotter past, and the equations of general relativity converge on a moment of infinite density and vanishing volume — a singularity at which the very theory that delivered us there ceases to mean anything. The map, followed faithfully, dissolves at its own origin.

We are accustomed to treating this as a problem of physics, to be solved by some future theory of quantum gravity that will smooth the singularity into something tractable. But the difficulty is older and stranger than any particular equation, and it does not obviously yield to better equations. It is the difficulty of asking where things come from when the asking itself is one of the things.

The receding floor

Consider what an explanation of a beginning must do. To say that the universe began with the hot, dense state we call the Big Bang is to describe a condition — fields at certain values, a spacetime of a certain curvature, laws of a certain form — and then to narrate what followed from it. The narration is the easy part; the equations are time-symmetric and run forward gladly. The hard part is the condition itself. For the explanation to explain, the initial state must be given, and whatever is given is, by definition, not accounted for by the explanation that begins there.

This is not a flaw in cosmology but a structural feature of explanation. Every account proceeds from premises it does not establish. To explain the Big Bang by a prior inflationary epoch is to inherit the inflaton field and its potential as new data; to explain those by a quantum fluctuation is to presuppose the quantum laws within which fluctuation is defined, and the strange tenseless arena in which "before" loses its footing. The floor recedes as we step onto it. What we call a beginning is only the place where, for reasons of evidence or imagination, our questioning happens to stop.

The question that includes itself

There is a temptation, here, to reach for the oldest of the metaphysical questions: why is there something rather than nothing? It has the cadence of profundity. But notice that the question smuggles in exactly what is at issue. "Why" asks for a reason, and a reason is a thing — a law, a ground, a fact about how matters stand. To answer why there is something is to produce a something, a principle from which the rest follows, and so to have failed to reach the nothing one was contrasting with. The nothing was never available as a starting point; it was a rhetorical foil, an absence we can name but not occupy.

The recursion bites hardest when we observe that the inquirer is internal to the inquiry. We are not surveying origins from some platform outside of time and law. We are configurations of matter that the early universe made possible, asking, with concepts the same history equipped us to form, after the conditions of our own asking. Causation, time, sufficient reason — the very tools with which we interrogate the beginning — are features of the world whose beginning is in question. To use them to reach behind the world is like trying to see the back of one's own eye.

An origin is not the first link in the chain but the point at which the chain is asked to hold its own weight, and finds nothing to hang from.

This does not condemn cosmology to silence, nor reduce its findings to fiction. The reconstructed history is real knowledge, hard-won and predictive, and the singularity may yet be dissolved into a smooth quantum prelude. What cannot be dissolved is the recursion. Whatever the prelude, it too will be a given, and the question — where does it come from, including the law by which it comes — will reconvene at its edge, patient and undiminished.

Living at the edge

Perhaps the lesson is not that the question is unanswerable but that we have mistaken its form. We have treated "origin" as a coordinate, a furthest point we might in principle reach, when it functions instead as a limit — the horizon past which our concepts no longer carry. A limit is not a failure of vision; it is the shape vision has. One does not lament that the visual field has an edge, as though a wider eye would abolish it. The edge is constitutive.

To think clearly about beginnings, then, is to learn to dwell at that edge without pretending to have crossed it — to hold the difference between explaining within the world and explaining the world, and to notice the second collapse quietly into the first each time we forget. The cosmologists have given us the most exact creation story our species has possessed, accurate to a fraction of a second after a moment that, examined, refuses to be a moment at all. That the story has no first page is not its defect. It is what it is honestly telling us: that every account of where anything comes from is told from inside the thing, by the thing, in words the thing supplied, and that the asking will always arrive one step too late to witness its own beginning.
