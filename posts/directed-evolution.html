The Ethics of Directed Evolution

Gene drives and synthetic biology hand us the controls of evolution itself. A framework for the responsibility that arrives with that power.

In 2018, biologists at Imperial College London reported that they had pushed a caged population of Anopheles gambiae mosquitoes to extinction. They had not poisoned the insects, nor starved them. They had instead inserted a gene that biased its own inheritance, a fragment of DNA engineered to copy itself onto the partner chromosome during reproduction so that, in defiance of Mendel's even-handed lottery, nearly every offspring inherited it. The chosen target was a gene called doublesex, which governs sexual development; disrupted, it rendered females sterile and deformed of mouth. Generation by generation the construct swept through the cage until no fertile female remained, and the population collapsed under the weight of an instruction it had been built to carry.

The technique is called a gene drive, and it inverts the oldest assumption of evolutionary thinking. Natural selection edits a lineage from the outside, by differential death and reproduction; a gene drive edits it from within, by rigging the dice of heredity itself. A harmful trait that selection would ordinarily purge can now be made to propagate precisely because it spreads faster than selection can remove it. For the first time, we possess a tool whose explicit purpose is to drive a chosen variation through a wild population without our continued involvement, releasing a self-perpetuating decision into the genome of a species.

The Inversion of Agency

What unsettles here is not the magnitude of the power but its grammar. Earlier interventions in nature were, in a sense, transactions: we cleared a forest, bred a wheat, loosed a cane toad, and the world answered with consequences we then had to meet. The intervention and its aftermath belonged to the same hand. A gene drive severs that continuity. The releasing generation acts; a hundred generations downstream inherit the act, with no capacity to consent to it and no author present to amend it. We are accustomed to thinking of evolution as the paradigm of an unauthored process, vast and indifferent. To author it is to assume a role that has never before had an occupant, and the role comes without a script.

The familiar ethical vocabularies strain against this. Consequentialism asks us to weigh outcomes, but the outcomes of a self-propagating change in a shared biosphere recede past any horizon we can survey; the ledger never closes. Rights-based reasoning asks whose consent is owed, and finds no one to ask, since the affected parties are unborn organisms, unborn humans, and ecosystems that do not deliberate. The very feature that makes the technology powerful, its independence from our ongoing supervision, is the feature that disables the frameworks we would use to govern it.

The Weight of the Irreversible

One response is to insist on reversibility. Engineers have proposed reversal drives, designed to overwrite a previous construct and restore the original sequence, and daisy-chain drives that exhaust themselves after a fixed number of generations, spending their momentum like a clock unwinding. These are genuine advances, and they matter. But they answer a narrower question than the one that should detain us. A drive can be reversed at the level of sequence while remaining irreversible at the level of the world: a species suppressed for three seasons does not slide back into the niche it vacated, and the predators that turned elsewhere, the plants that went unpollinated, the equilibria that resettled, do not unhappen because a corrective construct restores a letter of code. Reversibility of the cause is not reversibility of the effect.

To act where the consequence outlives every witness is to make a promise on behalf of people who cannot hold us to it.

This is the structure of the situation, and it is worth naming plainly. The technology does not present us with a harder version of a familiar problem. It presents us with a new kind of relation between an agent and time, in which the moment of choice and the duration of its effect have come radically apart. Ethics as we have practiced it assumes a rough proportion between the two: I act, I observe, I adjust. A gene drive dissolves that proportion. It asks the present to legislate for a future it will never see, and to do so with finality.

A Custodial Ethic

If the inherited frameworks falter, what remains is not paralysis but a change of register. The pertinent question shifts from what should we do to what kind of agent should we be when our actions outrun our knowledge. The relevant virtue is custodial rather than heroic: a disposition to act, when we must, as trustees of a future that cannot instruct us, holding our power under a standing presumption against the irreversible. This does not forbid the work. Malaria kills a child roughly every minute, and a sterile mosquito is not a small mercy. It demands instead that the licence to proceed be earned by the seriousness with which we acknowledge what we cannot undo.

The deepest temptation of directed evolution is to imagine that mastery of the mechanism is mastery of the outcome, that to control the gene is to control the world the gene will enter. It is not. We have acquired the power to write into the future and lost none of our incapacity to read it. The mosquito in the cage carried its instruction faithfully to the last generation; what it could not carry was any knowledge of the cage's edge, beyond which the world it would alter had never agreed to be remade.
