Fine-Tuning and Anthropic Reasoning

The constants of nature appear improbably hospitable to life. Design, selection, illusion, or the quiet tautology of our own existence?

That the cosmos permits us to ask whether it was made for us is the strangest of its hospitalities, and the least conclusive.

There is a number, written in the standard models of physics, that should not be where it is. The energy of empty space — the cosmological constant — is, by the reckoning of quantum field theory, expected to take a value roughly a hundred and twenty orders of magnitude larger than what we observe. Had it been even modestly larger, space would have expanded so violently that no galaxy, no star, no chemistry, and so no observer, could ever have formed. It sits instead at a value almost indistinguishable from zero, and we, improbably, are here to remark on it.

The cosmological constant is the most vertiginous case, but it is not alone. The strength of the strong nuclear force, the ratio of the electron's mass to the proton's, the relative feebleness of gravity — each appears to fall within a narrow band outside of which complexity, and therefore life, seems forbidden. The physicist Fred Hoyle, contemplating the resonance in carbon-12 that allows stars to forge the element on which all known biochemistry depends, remarked that the universe looked like a put-up job. The question this essay circles is not whether the appearance is real. It is what, if anything, the appearance is evidence for.

The shape of the inference

The temptation is to reason as one reasons about a watch found on a heath: fineness of fit implies a fitter. But the inference from improbability to design has a flaw that is easy to state and hard to dislodge. A fact that must obtain before any observer can exist cannot, by being observed, count as a surprising discovery. We could not have found ourselves in a universe inhospitable to finding. The constants are not a sample drawn at random from the space of possible constants and then checked for friendliness; they are the only constants we were ever in a position to measure.

This is the weak anthropic principle, and at first hearing it sounds like an evasion — a refusal to be astonished where astonishment seems owed. But it is not an answer to the question of why the constants are hospitable. It is a constraint on what can serve as an answer. It tells us that our own existence is not an additional clue, because it was a precondition of there being any clue-gatherer at all. The observation selects the observed. To treat our presence as confirming a hypothesis is to forget that the hypothesis had to be true for us to entertain it.

Tautology and its discontents

Some have wished to stop here, declaring the puzzle dissolved. We exist; therefore the universe permits existence; therefore there is nothing further to explain. But this is too quick, and the dissatisfaction it leaves is not mere stubbornness. Consider the man before a firing squad of expert marksmen who, the volley fired, finds himself alive. It is true that, were he dead, he could not be reflecting on his survival. The selection effect is real. Yet he is surely entitled to wonder why every rifle missed — to suspect a conspiracy, a planted blank, an arrangement. The bare fact of his survival explains why he is in a position to ask; it does not explain why there was anything to survive.

This is the residue the tautology cannot absorb. That observers find conditions suitable for observers is necessary. That suitable conditions should obtain at all, given how narrow they appear, is not. The anthropic principle accounts for the correlation between our existence and the world's friendliness; it is silent on the friendliness itself.

Many worlds, or one improbable fact

Modern cosmology offers a way to convert the apparent miracle into a selection effect after all. If the constants vary across an immense ensemble of universes — the eternal inflation of a landscape with some unthinkable number of vacua — then somewhere, inevitably, the dials read as ours do, and only there does anyone arise to read them. The marksmen fired a billion billion times; we are the survivor who lived to count. The improbable becomes the merely local.

The maneuver is elegant and may even be true, but its philosophical cost should be named. It purchases the dissolution of one mystery by positing an unobserved and perhaps unobservable infinity, trading a fine-tuned cosmos for a profligate one. It also leaves untouched a deeper asymmetry: that there should be laws at all admitting of tuning, a logical space of universes structured enough for some to bear observers. We have explained why we find ourselves among the winners without explaining why there was a lottery, or why its rules were such that winning was possible.

What the fine-tuning debate finally exposes is the limit of a certain kind of explanation — the kind that accounts for a fact by absorbing it into a larger pattern. Every such absorption leaves a remainder, and the remainder is always the existence of the pattern itself. We are not, in the end, astonished that the universe contains us. We are astonished that it is the sort of thing that could.
