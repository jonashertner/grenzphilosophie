Consciousness as Information Integration

Integrated Information Theory identifies consciousness with integrated information. On Φ, the difficulty of measuring it, and what it would mean for artificial minds.

Among the questions that survive every fashion in the philosophy of mind, the hardest is also the simplest to state. Why is there something it is like to be a thing that sees red, tastes salt, or dreads Monday? The functional story — light strikes the retina, signals propagate, behavior follows — can be told end to end without ever invoking the felt quality of the experience. The feeling seems to ride atop the mechanism, unbidden and unexplained. Integrated Information Theory, developed by the neuroscientist Giulio Tononi and elaborated with Christof Koch and others, is among the few proposals bold enough to attempt a quantity where most offer only a vocabulary.

Its move is to reverse the usual order of inquiry. Rather than start from the brain and ask which of its activities produce consciousness, the theory starts from the felt character of experience itself and asks what any physical system must be like to possess it. Experience, the theory observes, is intrinsic, structured, specific, unified, and definite. From these axioms it derives postulates about the cause-effect structure a mechanism must instantiate, and from those it extracts a single number.

The number and its meaning

That number is Φ, phi. It measures integrated information: the degree to which a system's repertoire of cause-effect power exceeds the sum of the power of its parts taken separately. A system has high Φ when partitioning it — cutting it into pieces and severing the influence each piece exerts on the rest — destroys information that the whole possessed and the fragments cannot recover. The whole, in the precise sense the theory makes available, is more than its parts; consciousness, the theory claims, simply is this irreducibility, viewed from within.

The claim is not that integrated information correlates with consciousness, the way blood oxygenation correlates with neural activity in a scanner. The claim is identity. Where there is integrated information of a certain structure, there is experience, necessarily, and the structure of the one is the character of the other. A grid of logic gates wired to integrate has a dim inner life; a photodiode, on the strictest reading, has the faintest glimmer of one. The theory wears its panpsychist consequences openly rather than apologizing for them.

Why the measure resists us

The difficulty is that Φ, as defined, is very nearly impossible to compute. To find it one must consider every possible way of partitioning the system and identify the partition that does least violence to its integration — the cut across which the system is most nearly whole. The number of partitions grows super-exponentially with the number of elements, so that for any system as large as a human brain the exact quantity lies beyond the reach of any conceivable computation. Practitioners work with proxies, approximations, and measures that share the spirit of Φ while abandoning its letter.

This is not a temporary embarrassment to be cleared up by faster machines. It exposes something about what the theory is asking. A quantity that determines whether and how much a system feels, yet which no system can in practice ascertain about another, occupies a strange epistemic position. It is objective in principle and inaccessible in fact.

We are asked to believe in a property as real as mass and as hidden as the soul it was meant to replace.

One might say the same of many physical quantities we never measure directly. But mass announces itself in weight, in inertia, in the bending of light. Φ, by the theory's own logic, need announce itself in no behavior at all. Two systems identical in every action they perform may differ in Φ, and so differ in whether anyone is home. The measure floats free of the very evidence we would use to test it.

The artificial case

Here the theory says something genuinely unwelcome, and to its credit refuses to soften it. A digital computer simulating a brain, neuron for neuron, would reproduce every output the brain produces and yet, because of how its transistors are physically organized — feed-forward, modular, their integration shallow — might have a Φ near zero. The simulation would speak of its feelings and have none. Functional equivalence, the lodestar of artificial intelligence, would be no guarantee of experience whatever.

This inverts the anxiety of the age. We have learned to fear machines that feel and are denied moral standing. The theory raises the opposite specter: machines that converse, console, and persuade with perfect fluency while the lights inside are out, and other machines, mute and inert and architecturally deep, in which something might nonetheless flicker. Whether a system matters morally would turn not on what it does but on a hidden geometry of its causes, a geometry no conversation could reveal.

It may be that Integrated Information Theory is wrong; many think it untestable, which for some is worse. But its provocation is durable independent of its truth. It insists that the question of who feels and who merely behaves is a question with a determinate answer, written into the physical structure of things, and that we may build minds, or fail to, without ever being permitted to know which we have done.
