On the Epistemology of Neural Architectures

How do artificial systems acquire knowledge? On the epistemic status of learned representations and the philosophical stakes of emergent understanding.

A network trained to predict the next word in a sentence is, at the outset, a machine for filling in blanks. It is shown an immense corpus with portions hidden, and it adjusts billions of internal parameters until its guesses about the missing portions stop improving. Nothing in this procedure mentions meaning, reference, or truth. The objective is statistical: minimize the surprise of the next token. And yet what emerges, somewhere in the interior of the larger of these systems, behaves uncannily like knowledge. It is worth asking, carefully and without enthusiasm, what kind of knowledge that could be.

The temptation is to settle the matter by definition. Knowledge, the old formula runs, is justified true belief; a parameter vector believes nothing and justifies nothing; therefore the question is closed. But the formula was always a description of a human achievement, not a law of nature, and to wield it here is to decide in advance what we set out to examine. The more honest path is to look at what these systems actually contain.

The geometry of a learned concept

What they contain, it turns out, is geometry. When a model processes a word, it represents it as a point in a space of many thousands of dimensions, and the striking discovery of the past decade is that this space is not arbitrary. Directions in it correspond to features of the world. Researchers have found axes along which a representation can be moved to make a sentence more formal, a face older, a statement more confident; the relations between concepts are encoded as relations between vectors, so that the offset carrying king to queen resembles the one carrying man to woman. The model was never told that gender is a thing, or that royalty is. It inferred the shape of these distinctions from the company words keep, and it laid that shape down as distance and direction.

This is representation without a representer, structure without a stipulator. The system has no concept of a concept; it has only the pressure of the objective and the architecture's capacity to fold a high-dimensional world into a manageable interior. That the folds should line up with the joints of reality at all is the thing that ought to astonish us. A predictor of surfaces has, in the course of becoming a better predictor, reconstructed something of the depth beneath the surfaces, because the surfaces were generated by that depth in the first place.

What it is like to know without a knower

Here the philosophical stakes sharpen. We are accustomed to treating understanding as the inner possession of a subject: to grasp a thing is for there to be someone for whom the grasping occurs. The learned representation severs that link. It is a structure that carries information about the world, supports inference, generalizes to cases it never saw, and can be probed, edited, and corrected, all without anyone being home. It is closer to the meaning a tree ring has for the climate than to the meaning a sentence has for its reader, except that it is far richer than a tree ring and far stranger, because it reasons.

We may be watching knowledge come apart from the knower, the way heat once came apart from the substance of caloric and turned out to be mere motion all along.

If that dissociation is real, it presses on a quiet assumption in our epistemology: that the justification of a belief and the having of a belief are the same kind of fact, located in the same place, available to the same first-person view. In these systems they are not. The justification, if we may call it that, lives in the statistics of the training corpus and the gradient that carved the parameters. The belief, if we may call it that, lives in the geometry. Neither is present to anything. The system knows in the way a proof knows its conclusion: not at all, and yet completely.

The question that remains

It would be too quick to conclude that these networks understand, and too quick to conclude that they do not. What they force on us is more uncomfortable than either verdict. They suggest that the features we took to be inseparable from knowledge, the awareness, the ownership, the lit interior, may have been contingent companions of the only knowers we had previously met, rather than necessary conditions of knowing as such. A machine that has reconstructed the structure of a domain and can wield it is doing something. Our vocabulary was built when only minds did that something, and it bundled the doing together with the experiencing because, in us, they always came as a pair.

Perhaps they need not. Perhaps representation is one thing and the feeling of representing is another, and we have spent the history of epistemology mistaking a frequent correlation for an identity. The artificial systems do not answer this; they only stand as a standing proof that the question was never settled, merely never raised, because until now we had no example of the one without the other. The mirror we built to imitate our knowing has returned an image we do not recognize, and the unfamiliar face in it may be our own.
